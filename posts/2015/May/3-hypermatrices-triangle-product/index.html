The Triangle Product and 3-Hypermatrices

Date Wed 06 May 2015 By M.E. Irizarry-Gelpí Category Maths Tags hypermatrix

Lately I have been thinking a lot about linear algebra and matrices. Indeed, I have been thinking about higher-dimensional generalizations involving $p \times q \times r$ 3-hypermatrices. This was triggered by reading this blog post on tensors. First, let me review the story of $d$-vectors and $(m \times n)$-matrices.

0-Hypermatrices

0-Hypermatrices are scalars. These objects carry no indices and can be represented by just a single (real or complex) number.

1-Hypermatrices

1-Hypermatrices are vectors. These objects carry one index and can be represented as linear arrays of numbers. A $d$-vector with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{d}$.

2-Hypermatrices

2-Hypermatrices are traditional matrices. These objects carry two indices and can be represented as rectangular arrays of numbers. An $(m \times n)$-matrix with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$. You have the matrix product function $P$ that takes an $(m \times p)$-matrix and a $(p \times n)$-matrix, and returns an $(m \times n)$-matrix: $$P: \mathbb{R}^{m \times p} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{p \times n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}.$$ This product function has two important properties:

  1. It is linear in both variables.
  2. It results in another rectangular matrix.

I will refer to this product function as the line product function.

You have the transpose function $T$ that takes an $(m \times n)$-matrix, and returns an $(n \times m)$-matrix: $$T: \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}.$$ The transpose function satisfies the property $T(T(A)) = A$ for any $(m \times n)$-matrix $A$.

There are two ways to describe a $d$-vector as a matrix: either as a $(d \times 1)$-matrix (a column vector), or as an $(1 \times d)$-matrix (a row vector). Let $U$ be a $(d \times 1)$-matrix and $W$ be a $(1 \times d)$-matrix. Then $P(U, V)$ is a $(d \times d)$-matrix (the outer product), and $P(V, U)$ is a scalar (the inner product).

A square $n$-matrix is an $(n \times n)$-matrix. The $n$-identity matrix is a very important square $n$-matrix.

3-Hypermatrices

3-Hypermatrices are the next step in the ladder. These objects carry three indices and can be represented as a cuboidal array of numbers. A $(p \times q \times r)$-hypermatrix with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{p \times q \times r}$.

Since a hypermatrix has three indices, the line product function no longer returns another hypermatrix. After treating a hypermatrix as a triangle, and noticing that you can make a triangle from three other triangles, I arrived at the triangle product function: $$\Delta : \mathbb{R}^{a \times p \times q} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{p \times b \times r} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{q \times r \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c}.$$ In index form, this looks like $$D_{abc} = A_{apq} B_{pbr} C_{qrc}.$$ This product function shares the same two properties from above with the line product function.

Before I introduce the five transpose functions, it is useful to introduce to auxiliary functions: $$S: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{c \times a \times b}, \quad M: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{c \times b \times a},$$ which satisfy the properties $S(S(S(A))) = A$ and $M(M(A)) = A$ for any $(a \times b \times c)$-hypermatrix $A$. With these in hand, the five transpose functions are: $$S, \quad S \circ S, \quad M, \quad S \circ M, \quad S \circ S \circ M.$$ Together with the identity function, these six operations form the smallest nonabelian group, the dihedral group of order 6.

There are three ways to describe a $d$-vector as a hypermatrix: either as a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, as a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, or as a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix. Let $X$ be a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, $Y$ be a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, and $Z$ be a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix. Then:

  • $\Delta(X, Y, Z)$ is a $(d \times d \times d)$-hypermatrix, $X_{a} Y_{b} Z_{c}$
  • $\Delta(X, Z, Y)$ is a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, $(Z \cdot Y) X_{a}$
  • $\Delta(Z, Y, X)$ is a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, $(Z \cdot X) Y_{b}$
  • $\Delta(Y, X, Z)$ is a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix, $(Y \cdot X) Z_{c}$

Note that $\Delta(Z, X, Y)$ and $\Delta(Y, Z, X)$ are not well-defined.
