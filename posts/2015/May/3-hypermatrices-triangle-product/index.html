The Triangle Product and 3-Hypermatrices

Date Wed 06 May 2015 By M.E. Irizarry-Gelpí Category Maths Tags hypermatrix

Lately I have been thinking a lot about linear algebra and matrices. Indeed, I have been thinking about higher-dimensional generalizations involving $p \times q \times r$ 3-hypermatrices. This was triggered by reading this blog post on tensors. First, let me review the story of $d$-vectors and $(m \times n)$-matrices.

0-Hypermatrices

0-Hypermatrices are scalars. These objects carry no indices and can be represented by just a single (real or complex) number.

1-Hypermatrices

1-Hypermatrices are vectors. These objects carry one index and can be represented as linear arrays of numbers. A $d$-vector with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{d}$.

2-Hypermatrices

2-Hypermatrices are traditional matrices. These objects carry two indices and can be represented as rectangular arrays of numbers. An $(m \times n)$-matrix with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$.

You have the matrix product function $P$ that takes an $(m \times p)$-matrix and a $(p \times n)$-matrix, and returns an $(m \times n)$-matrix: $$P: \mathbb{R}^{m \times p} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{p \times n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}.$$ This product function has two important properties:

  1. It is linear in both variables.
  2. It results in another rectangular matrix.

I will refer to this product function as the line product function.

You have the transpose function $T$ that takes an $(m \times n)$-matrix, and returns an $(n \times m)$-matrix: $$T: \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}.$$ The transpose function satisfies the property $T(T(A)) = A$ for any $(m \times n)$-matrix $A$.

There are two ways to describe a $d$-vector as a matrix: either as a $(d \times 1)$-matrix (a column vector), or as an $(1 \times d)$-matrix (a row vector). Let $U$ be a $(d \times 1)$-matrix and $W$ be a $(1 \times d)$-matrix. Then $P(U, V)$ is a $(d \times d)$-matrix (the outer product), and $P(V, U)$ is a scalar (the inner product).

An $n$-square matrix is an $(n \times n)$-matrix. The $n$-identity matrix is an important example of a $n$-square matrix. The line product of two $n$-square matrices $A$ and $B$ is another $n$-square matrix. However, in general $P(A, B) \neq P(B, A)$. Indeed, the commutator function $[A, B]$ quantifies this discrepancy: $$[A, B] = P(A, B) - P(B, A).$$ The line product function is noncommutative.

3-Hypermatrices

3-Hypermatrices are the next step in the ladder. These objects carry three indices and can be represented as a cuboidal array of numbers. A $(p \times q \times r)$-hypermatrix with real components is an element of $\mathbb{R}^{p \times q \times r}$.

Since a 3-hypermatrix has three indices, the line product function no longer returns another 3-hypermatrix. After treating the index structure of a 3-hypermatrix as a triangle, and noticing that you can make a triangle from three other triangles, I arrived at the triangle product function: $$\Delta : \mathbb{R}^{a \times p \times q} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{p \times b \times r} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{q \times r \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c}.$$ In index form, this looks like $$\Delta_{abc} = A_{apq} B_{pbr} C_{qrc}.$$ This product function shares the same two properties from above with the line product function.

Before I introduce the five transpose functions that can act on a 3-hypermatrix, it is useful to introduce two auxiliary functions: $$R: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{c \times a \times b}, \quad M: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{c \times b \times a},$$ which satisfy the properties $R(R(R(A))) = A$ and $M(M(A)) = A$ for any $(a \times b \times c)$-hypermatrix $A$. With these in hand, the five transpose functions are: $$R, \quad R \circ R, \quad M, \quad R \circ M, \quad R \circ R \circ M.$$ Together with the identity function $I$, these six operations form the smallest nonabelian group, the dihedral group of order 6. Alternatively, you can introduce two swap functions: $$S_{12}: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{b \times a \times c}, \quad S_{23}: \mathbb{R}^{a \times b \times c} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{a \times c \times b}. $$ which satisfy $S_{12}(S_{12}(A)) = A$ and $S_{23}(S_{23}(A)) = A$. Then the five transpose functions are $$S_{12}, \quad S_{23}, \quad S_{12} \circ S_{23}, \quad S_{23} \circ S_{12}, \quad S_{12} \circ S_{23} \circ S_{12}. $$ Note that $S_{12} \circ S_{23} = R$ and $S_{12} \circ S_{23} \circ S_{12} = M$. If the entries of the 3-hypermatrix are complex, you can enhance the transposition group by adding a complex conjugation function $C$ and five hermitian transpose functions. Thus, the hermitian transposition group has order 12.

There are three ways to describe a $d$-vector as a 3-hypermatrix: either as a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, as a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, or as a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix. Let $X$ be a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, $Y$ be a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, and $Z$ be a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix. Then:

  • $\Delta(X, Y, Z)$ is a $(d \times d \times d)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the polyadic tensor $X_{a} Y_{b} Z_{c}$
  • $\Delta(X, Z, Y)$ is a $(d \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the vector $(Z \cdot Y) X_{a}$
  • $\Delta(Z, Y, X)$ is a $(1 \times d \times 1)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the vector $(Z \cdot X) Y_{b}$
  • $\Delta(Y, X, Z)$ is a $(1 \times 1 \times d)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the vector $(Y \cdot X) Z_{c}$

Note that $\Delta(Z, X, Y)$ and $\Delta(Y, Z, X)$ are not well-defined.

There are three ways to describe a $n$-square matrix as a 3-hypermatrix: either as a $(1 \times n \times n)$-hypermatrix, as an $(n \times 1 \times n)$-hypermatrix, or as an $(n \times n \times 1)$-hypermatrix. Let $F$ be a $(1 \times n \times n)$-hypermatrix, $G$ be a $(n \times 1 \times n)$-hypermatrix, and $H$ be a $(n \times n \times 1)$-hypermatrix. Then:

  • $\Delta(F, G, H)$ is an $(1 \times 1 \times 1)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the scalar $\operatorname{tr}{(F G H)}$
  • $\Delta(F, H, G)$ is an $(1 \times n \times n)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the $n$-square matrix $\operatorname{tr}{(HG)} F_{ab}$
  • $\Delta(H, G, F)$ is an $(n \times 1 \times n)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the $n$-square matrix $\operatorname{tr}{(HF)} G_{ab}$
  • $\Delta(G, F, H)$ is an $(n \times n \times 1)$-hypermatrix, i.e. the $n$-square matrix $\operatorname{tr}{(FG)} H_{ab}$

Note that $\Delta(H, F, G)$ and $\Delta(G, H, F)$ are not well-defined.

An $n$-cube 3-hypermatrix is an $(n \times n \times n)$-hypermatrix. The $n$-identity 3-hypermatrix is an important example of an $n$-cube 3-hypermatrix. The triangle product of three $n$-cube 3-hypermatrices $A$, $B$ and $C$ is another $n$-cube 3-hypermatrix.
