Gram Momentum Invariants

Date Thu 28 August 2014 By M.E. Irizarry-Gelpí Category Physics Tags kinematics

In this post I will mention some facts about what I call Gram momentum invariants. I will assume that every energy-momentum vector $p_{I}$ satisfies an on-shell constraint of the form $$p_{I}^{2} = - m_{I}^{2}$$ with the mass $m_{I}$ being real and positive.

Introduction

In a previous post I introduced the Mandelstam momentum invariants. We can construct $n$-Mandelstam invariants with $n$ distinct energy-momentum vectors. With a similar collection of $n$ distinct energy-momentum vectors, we construct an $n$-Gram invariant: $$G_{I_{1} I_{2} \cdots I_{n}} = \det{ \begin{pmatrix} p_{I_{1}} \cdot p_{I_{1}} & \cdots & p_{I_{1}} \cdot p_{I_{n}} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ p_{I_{1}} \cdot p_{I_{n}} & \cdots & p_{I_{n}} \cdot p_{I_{n}} \end{pmatrix} }.$$ That is, an $n$-Gram invariant correspond to the Gram determinant associated with a collection of $n$ energy-momentum vectors. Hence, by construction, the Gram momentum invariants are sensitive to collinearity of the external energy-momentum vectors (i.e. they vanish whenever a pair of energy-momentum vectors are collinear).

Two Quanta

Given two linearly-independent energy-momentum vectors, the 2-Gram invariant is $$G_{IJ} \equiv \det{ \begin{pmatrix} p_{I}^{2} & p_{I} \cdot p_{J} \\ p_{I} \cdot p_{J} & p_{J}^{2} \end{pmatrix} } = p_{I}^{2} p_{J}^{2} - (p_{I} \cdot p_{J})^{2}.$$ Using $$p_{I}^{2} = - m_{I}^{2} \qquad (p_{I} + p_{J})^{2} = -s_{IJ} \qquad p_{J}^{2} = - m_{J}^{2}$$ we can write $$p_{I} \cdot p_{J} = \frac{m_{I}^{2} + m_{J}^{2} - s_{IJ}}{2}$$ This allows me to write the 2-Gram invariant in terms of masses and 2-Mandelstam invariants: $$G_{IJ} = \frac{1}{4} [s_{IJ} - (m_{I} - m_{J})^{2} ] [ (m_{I} + m_{J})^{2} - s_{IJ}].$$ In this form, the sign properties of a 2-Gram invariant are obvious. We have $G_{IJ}$ being negative whenever $s_{IJ} < (m_{I} - m_{J})^{2}$ or $s_{IJ} > (m_{I} + m_{J})^{2}$. Part of this region lies above the 2-threshold, and another part lies below the 2-pseudothreshold. On the other hand, we have $G_{IJ}$ being positive whenever $(m_{I} - m_{J})^{2} < s_{IJ} < (m_{I} + m_{J})^{2}$. All of this region lies below the 2-threshold and above the 2-pseudothreshold.

Three Quanta

Given three linearly-independent energy-momentum vectors, the 3-Gram invariant is $$G_{IJK} \equiv \det{ \begin{pmatrix} p_{I}^{2} & p_{I} \cdot p_{J} & p_{I} \cdot p_{K} \\ p_{I} \cdot p_{J} & p_{J}^{2} & p_{J} \cdot p_{K} \\ p_{I} \cdot p_{K} & p_{J} \cdot p_{K} & p_{K}^{2} \end{pmatrix} } = p_{I}^{2} p_{J}^{2} p_{K}^{2} - p_{I}^{2} (p_{J} \cdot p_{K}) - p_{J}^{2} (p_{I} \cdot p_{K}) - p_{K}^{2} (p_{I} \cdot p_{J}) + 2 (p_{I} \cdot p_{J})(p_{I} \cdot p_{K})(p_{J} \cdot p_{K}) .$$ In general, this cannot be simplified further in terms of masses and Mandelstam invariants.
