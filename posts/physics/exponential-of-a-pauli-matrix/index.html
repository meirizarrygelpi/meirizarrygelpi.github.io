Exponential of a Pauli Matrix

  • Tue 06 February 2018
  • Physics
  • #8.370.1x

The three Pauli matrices are

$$ X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad Y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad Z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}. $$

Each is Hermitian and square to the identity:

$$ X^{2} = Y^{2} = Z^{2} = I. $$

Consider the following function:

$$ U_{M}(\theta) \equiv \exp{\left( \frac{i}{2} \theta M \right)}, \qquad M^{2} = I. $$

The exponential of a matrix is defined in terms of the infinite series

$$ U_{M}(\theta) = \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\Gamma(n+1)} \left( \frac{i}{2} \theta M \right)^{n}. $$

First, you split the sum into even and odd powers:

$$ U_{M}(\theta) = \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\Gamma(2n+1)} \left( \frac{i}{2} \theta M \right)^{2n} + \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\Gamma(2n+2)} \left( \frac{i}{2} \theta M \right)^{2n + 1}. $$

Then you use the fact that

$$ i^{2n} = (-1)^{n}, \qquad M^{2n} = I; $$

to find

$$ U_{M}(\theta) = I \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{\Gamma(2n+1)} \left( \frac{1}{2} \theta \right)^{2n} + i M \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{\Gamma(2n+2)} \left( \frac{1}{2} \theta \right)^{2n + 1}. $$

Finally, you recognize the sine and cosine infinite series:

$$ U_{M}(\theta) = I \cos{\left( \frac{\theta}{2} \right)} + i M \sin{\left( \frac{\theta}{2} \right)}. $$

Here \(M\) can be any of the three Pauli matrices.

Recall also that the three Pauli matrices anti-commute:

$$ XY + YX = YZ + ZY = ZX + XZ = 0. $$

Now consider the following matrix

$$r \cdot R \equiv x X + y Y + z Z, \qquad x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2} = 1. $$

Then it follows that

$$ (r \cdot R)^{2} = I. $$

Thus, in general

$$ \exp{\left[ \frac{i}{2} \theta (r \cdot R) \right]} = I \cos{\left( \frac{\theta}{2} \right)} + i (r \cdot R) \sin{\left( \frac{\theta}{2} \right)}. $$

If you relax the unit 2-sphere condition, then

$$ (r \cdot R)^{2} = \left(\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}}\right)^{2} I; $$

and thus

$$ \exp{\left[ \frac{i}{2} \theta (r \cdot R) \right]} = I \cos{\left( \frac{\theta \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}}}{2} \right)} + i (r \cdot R) \sin{\left( \frac{\theta \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2}}}{2} \right)}. $$

In any case, the length of the \(r\) vector can always be absorbed into the angle \(\theta\).
