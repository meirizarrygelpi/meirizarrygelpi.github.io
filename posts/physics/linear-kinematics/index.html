Linear Kinematics

Date Fri 28 October 2016 By M.E. Irizarry-Gelpí Category Physics Tags kinematics

Recently I came across a neat result for a non-relativistic 2-to-2 elastic collision in one time plus one space dimensions. You have two kinds of particles. Each incoming particle has a momentum \(p\), a velocity \(u\), and kinetic energy. Each outgoing particle has a momentum \(q\), a velocity \(v\), and kinetic energy. Non-relativistically, momentum and velocity are related via

$$ p = m u, \qquad q = m v. $$

There is conservation of total linear momentum,

$$ p_{1} + p_{2} = q_{1} + q_{2} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad m_{1} u_{1} + m_{2} u_{2} = m_{1} v_{1} + m_{2} v_{2}. $$

Since this collision is elastic, the total kinetic energy is also conserved:

$$ m_{1} u_{1}^{2} + m_{2} u_{2}^{2} = m_{1} v_{1}^{2} + m_{2} v_{2}^{2} $$

Using these two constraints, you can solve for the outgoing velocities in terms of the incoming velocities. There are two sets of solutions. You can have the forward scattering case:

$$ v_{1} = u_{1}, \qquad v_{2} = u_{2}; $$

or the more complicated case:

$$ v_{1} = \frac{(m_{1} - m_{2}) u_{1} + 2 m_{2} u_{2}}{m_{1} + m_{2}} , \qquad v_{2} = \frac{(m_{2} - m_{1}) u_{2} + 2 m_{1} u_{1}}{m_{1} + m_{2}}. $$

When the masses are equal (\(m_{1} = m_{2} = m\)), the more complicated case reduces to backward scattering:

$$ v_{1} = u_{2}, \qquad v_{2} = u_{1}. $$

Note that in general you always have

$$ v_{1} - v_{2} = u_{2} - u_{1} $$

This particular equation is invariant under a constant shift in velocity, which corresponds to a Galileo transformation. This result is general and does not assume working in the center-of-mass frame.

Relativistic Case

In the relativistic case, each particle can have a different mass, so you can study the more general inelastic case and still have conservation of energy,

$$ E_{1} + E_{2} = E_{3} + E_{4}; $$

and conservation of momentum,

$$ p_{1} + p_{2} = p_{3} + p_{4}; $$

but each energy-momentum pair satisfies one of three possible on-shell constraints:

$$ -m^{2} = -E^{2} + p^{2}; $$
$$ 0 = -E^{2} + p^{2}; $$
$$ +w^{2} = -E^{2} + p^{2}. $$

The slow constraint can be solved by introducing a slow rapidity \(\xi\) via

$$ E = m \cosh{(\xi)}, \qquad p = m \sinh{(\xi)} $$

The slow velocity of each slow particle is given by

$$ v \equiv \frac{p}{E} = \tanh{(\xi)} $$

In terms of rapidity, the conservation constraints become

$$ m_{1} \cosh{(\xi_{1})} + m_{2} \cosh{(\xi_{2})} = m_{3} \cosh{(\xi_{3})} + m_{4} \cosh{(\xi_{4})}; $$

and

$$ m_{1} \sinh{(\xi_{1})} + m_{2} \sinh{(\xi_{2})} = m_{3} \sinh{(\xi_{3})} + m_{4} \sinh{(\xi_{4})}. $$

From the hyperbolic identities

$$ \cosh^{2}{(x)} - \sinh^{2}{(x)} = 1, $$
$$ \cosh{(x - y)} = \cosh{(x)} \cosh{(y)} - \sinh{(x)} \sinh{(y)}; $$

it follows that

$$ m_{1}^{2} + m_{2}^{2} + 2 m_{1}m_{2} \cosh{(\xi_{1} - \xi_{2})} = m_{3}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} + 2 m_{3}m_{4} \cosh{(\xi_{3} - \xi_{4})}. $$

It turns out that this relation secretly has to do with the 2-Mandelstam invariant \(s\). You can introduce three 2-Mandelstam invariants:

$$ s = -(P_{1} + P_{2})^{2} = -(P_{3} + P_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ t = -(P_{1} - P_{3})^{2} = -(P_{2} - P_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ u = -(P_{1} - P_{4})^{2} = -(P_{2} - P_{3})^{2}. $$

You can also introduce six Regge-Mandelstam invariants:

$$ r_{12} = \frac{s - m_{1}^{2} - m_{2}^{2}}{2 m_{1} m_{2}}, $$
$$ r_{34} = \frac{s - m_{3}^{2} - m_{4}^{2}}{2 m_{3} m_{4}}, $$
$$ r_{13} = \frac{m_{1}^{2} + m_{3}^{2} - t}{2 m_{1} m_{3}}, $$
$$ r_{24} = \frac{m_{2}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} - t}{2 m_{2} m_{4}}, $$
$$ r_{14} = \frac{m_{1}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} - u}{2 m_{1} m_{4}}, $$
$$ r_{23} = \frac{m_{2}^{2} + m_{3}^{2} - u}{2 m_{2} m_{3}}. $$

It follows that the Regge-Mandelstam invariants are simply

$$ r_{ij} = \cosh{(\xi_{i} - \xi_{j})}. $$

From these you can find two other relations:

$$ m_{1}^{2} + m_{3}^{2} - 2 m_{1} m_{3} \cosh{(\xi_{1} - \xi_{3})} = m_{2}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} - 2 m_{2} m_{4} \cosh{(\xi_{2} - \xi_{4})}, $$
$$ m_{1}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} - 2 m_{1} m_{4} \cosh{(\xi_{1} - \xi_{4})} = m_{2}^{2} + m_{3}^{2} - 2 m_{2} m_{3} \cosh{(\xi_{2} - \xi_{3})}. $$

Since the hyperbolic cosine is always larger than 1, you have conditions on all Regge-Mandelstam invariants

$$ r_{ij} \geq 1. $$

These conditions translate to

$$ s \geq (m_{1} + m_{2})^{2}, \qquad s \geq (m_{3} + m_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ t \leq (m_{1} - m_{3})^{2}, \qquad t \leq (m_{2} - m_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ u \leq (m_{1} - m_{4})^{2}, \qquad u \leq (m_{2} - m_{3})^{2}; $$

Another thing you can do is use the definitions

$$ \sinh{(x)} = \frac{\exp{(x)} - \exp{(-x)}}{2}, $$
$$ \cosh{(x)} = \frac{\exp{(x)} + \exp{(-x)}}{2}. $$

to find two relations:

$$ m_{1} \exp{(\xi_{1})} + m_{2} \exp{(\xi_{2})} = m_{3} \exp{(\xi_{3})} + m_{4} \exp{(\xi_{4})}, $$
$$ \frac{m_{1}}{\exp{(\xi_{1})}} + \frac{m_{2}}{\exp{(\xi_{2})}} = \frac{m_{3}}{\exp{(\xi_{3})}} + \frac{m_{4}}{\exp{(\xi_{4})}}. $$

Also,

$$ \exp{(2 \xi)} = \frac{1 + \tanh{(\xi)}}{1 - \tanh{(\xi)}} = \frac{1 + v}{1 - v}. $$

Slow-Fast Mixed Kinematics

Consider now the case where one of the incoming particles is slow, and the other is fast. Similarly for the outgoing particles. That is,

$$ E_{1} = w_{1} \sinh{(\rho_{1})}, \qquad p_{1} = w_{1} \cosh{(\rho_{1})}; $$
$$ E_{2} = m_{2} \cosh{(\xi_{2})}, \qquad p_{2} = m_{2} \sinh{(\xi_{2})}; $$
$$ E_{3} = w_{3} \sinh{(\rho_{3})}, \qquad p_{3} = w_{3} \cosh{(\rho_{3})}; $$
$$ E_{4} = m_{4} \cosh{(\xi_{4})}, \qquad p_{4} = m_{4} \sinh{(\xi_{4})}. $$

Using the same 2-Mandelstam invariants as above:

$$ s = -(P_{1} + P_{2})^{2} = -(P_{3} + P_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ t = -(P_{1} - P_{3})^{2} = -(P_{2} - P_{4})^{2}; $$
$$ u = -(P_{1} - P_{4})^{2} = -(P_{2} - P_{3})^{2}; $$

now gives

$$ \frac{s + w_{1}^{2} - m_{2}^{2}}{2 w_{1} m_{2}} = \sinh{(\rho_{1} - \xi_{2})}; $$
$$ \frac{s + w_{3}^{2} - m_{4}^{2}}{2 w_{3} m_{4}} = \sinh{(\rho_{3} - \xi_{4})}; $$
$$ \frac{t + w_{1}^{2} + w_{3}^{2}}{2 w_{1} w_{3}} = \cosh{(\rho_{1} - \rho_{3})}; $$
$$ \frac{m_{2}^{2} + m_{4}^{2} - t}{2 m_{2} m_{4}} = \cosh{(\xi_{2} - \xi_{4})}; $$
$$ \frac{m_{4}^{2} - w_{1}^{2} - u}{2 w_{1} m_{4}} = \sinh{(\rho_{1} - \xi_{4})}; $$
$$ \frac{u - m_{2}^{2} + w_{3}^{2}}{2 m_{2} w_{3}} = \sinh{(\xi_{2} - \rho_{3})}. $$

Since the hyperbolic cosine is always greater than 1, you have the condition

$$ -(w_{1} - w_{3})^{2} \leq t \leq (m_{2} - m_{4})^{2}. $$

Note that in the elastic case you have \(w_{1} = w_{3}\) and \(m_{2} = m_{4}\) and thus \(t = 0\).
