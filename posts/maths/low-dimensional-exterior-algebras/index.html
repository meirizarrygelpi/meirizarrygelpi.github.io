Low-Dimensional Exterior Algebras

Date Mon 12 June 2017 By M.E. Irizarry-Gelpí Category Maths Tags exterior-algebra / parabolic

The Cayley-Dickson construction can be generalized to yield other kinds of algebras other than quaternions and octonions. The parabolic constructs involve adjoining elements that square to zero. At first I thought that these constructs were the dual numbers (two-dimensional) and the hyper numbers (four-dimensional). It turns out that the parabolic Cayley-Dickson constructs are closer to exterior algebras.

Recall that an exterior algebra \(\Lambda(V)\) over an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\) is a direct sum of spaces:

$$ \Lambda(V) = \Lambda_{0}(V) \oplus \Lambda_{1}(V) \oplus \Lambda_{2}(V) \oplus \ldots \oplus \Lambda_{n}(V). $$

Here, \(\Lambda_{0}\) is the space of 0-forms, which in our case, are scalar real numbers; \(\Lambda_{1}\) is the space of 1-forms; \(\Lambda_{2}\) is the space of 2-forms, and in general, \(\Lambda_{k}\) is the space of \(k\)-forms (with \(0 \leq k \leq n\)). The 1-forms are like vectors. The 2-forms are obtained by multiplying two 1-forms with the wedge product. Given two 1-forms \(X\) and \(Y\), the wedge product

$$ X \wedge Y $$

is a 2-form. The wedge product of two 1-forms is anti-symmetric:

$$ Y \wedge X = - X \wedge Y. $$

In general, the dimension of \(\Lambda_{k}\) is the binomial coefficient:

$$ \dim{(\Lambda_{k})} = {n \choose k}. $$

Thus,

$$ \dim{(\Lambda)} = \sum_{k = 0}^{n} {n \choose k} = 2^{n}. $$

We are going to explore the cases \(n = 1\), \(n = 2\), \(n = 3\), and \(n = 4\).

One-Dimensional

In the one-dimensional case, the dimension of the exterior algebra is 2. This exterior algebra coincides with the dual numbers:

$$ \Lambda(\mathbb{R}) = \left\lbrace a + b W \mid (a, b) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \right\rbrace. $$

If \(x\) and \(y\) are elements of \(\Lambda\), then their wedge product is given by

$$ x \wedge y = (a_{1} + b_{1}W) \wedge (a_{2} + b_{2}W) = a_{1}a_{2} + (a_{1}b_{2} + b_{1}a_{2})W + b_{1}b_{2} (W \wedge W). $$

The third term is zero because of the anti-symmetry of the wedge product. However, note that in this one-dimensional case the multiplication is commutative:

$$ y \wedge x = (a_{2} + b_{2}W) \wedge (a_{1} + b_{1}W) = a_{2}a_{1} + (a_{2}b_{1} + b_{2} a_{1})W. $$

This is the only case when the multiplication is commutative.

Hodge Star

The Hodge star operation relates a \(k\)-form to an \((n - k)\)-form. In this case, it provides a relation between 0-forms and 1-forms. If

$$x = a + bW, $$

then the Hodge star operation on \(x\) is

$${}\star x = b + aW. $$

That is, the Hodge star operation swaps the real and unreal parts of \(x\). Note that

$${}\star ({}\star x) = x.$$

That is, in this case the Hodge star operation is an involution.

Note that

$$x \wedge ({} \star x) = ab + (a^{2} + b^{2})W.$$

Two-Dimensional

In the two-dimensional case, the dimension of the exterior algebra is 4. This algebra can be obtained by performing a parabolic Cayley-Dickson construct with the dual numbers. At first I thought that this lead to the hyper numbers. However, the nilpotent units in the hyper numbers commute. Because of the anti-symmetry of the wedge product, the nilpotent units in the exterior algebra anti-commute. The full algebra is:

$$ \Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2}) = \left\lbrace a + b W + c X + d (W \wedge X) \mid (a, b,c,d) \in \mathbb{R}^4 \right\rbrace. $$

You can make the Cayley-Dickson construct obvious by writing \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2})\) like

$$ \Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2}) = \left\lbrace A + B \wedge X \mid (A, B) \in \Lambda(\mathbb{R}) \times \Lambda(\mathbb{R}) \right\rbrace. $$

If \(x\) and \(y\) are elements of \(\Lambda\), then their wedge product is given by

$$ x \wedge y = \left[a_{1} + b_{1}W + c_{1}X + d_{1}(W \wedge X)\right] \wedge \left[a_{2} + b_{2}W + c_{2}X + d_{2}(W \wedge X)\right]. $$

More explicitly:

$$ x \wedge y = a_{1}a_{2} + (a_{1}b_{2} + b_{1}a_{2})W + (a_{1}c_{2} + c_{1}a_{2})X + (a_{1}d_{2} + d_{1}a_{2} + b_{1}c_{2} - c_{1}b_{2})(W \wedge X). $$

Because of the anti-symmetry of \(W \wedge X\), the product of two elements of \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2})\) is non-commutative. However, it is associative.

Hodge Star

The Hodge star operation relates a \(k\)-form to an \((n - k)\)-form. In this two-dimensional case, it provides a relation between 0-forms and 2-forms; and a relation between 1-forms:

$${}\star 1 = W \wedge X, \qquad {}\star W = X, \qquad {}\star X = - W, \qquad {}\star (W \wedge X) = 1.$$

Note that

$$ {}\star ({}\star 1) = 1, \qquad {}\star ({}\star (W \wedge X)) = W \wedge X; $$

but

$$ {}\star ({}\star W) = -W, \qquad {}\star ({}\star X) = -X. $$

To be specific, if

$$x = a + b W + c X + d (W \wedge X); $$

then the Hodge star operation on \(x\) is

$$ {}\star x = d - c W + b X + a (W \wedge X). $$

Thinking of \(x\) (an element of \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2})\)) as a construct with \(A\) and \(B\) (elements of \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R})\)) gives

$$x = A + B \wedge X, \qquad {}\star x = {}\star (\operatorname{Conj}(B)) + ({}\star A) \wedge X.$$

This is a way to express the two-dimensional Hodge star operation in terms of the one-dimensional Hodge star operation and the one-dimensional conjugation operation.

Acting a second time with the Hodge star gives

$${}\star ({}\star x) = a - b W - c X + d (W \wedge X). $$

Acting a third time gives

$${}\star ({}\star ({}\star x)) = d + c W - bY + a(W\wedge X). $$

Finally, acting a fourth time gives the identity operation

$${}\star ({}\star ({}\star ({}\star x))) = x. $$

Note that

$$ x \wedge ({} \star x) = ad - (ac - bd)W + (ab + cd)X + (a^{2} + b^{2} + c^{2} + d^{2})(W \wedge X). $$

Three-Dimensional

In the three-dimensional case, the dimension of the exterior algebra is 8. This algebra can be obtained by performing a parabolic Cayley-Dickson construct with \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{2})\). The full algebra is

$$ \Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{3}) = \lbrace a + bW + cY + d (W \wedge Y) + eZ + f (W \wedge Z) + g (Y \wedge Z) + h ((W \wedge Y) \wedge Z) \mid (a,b,c,d,e,f,g,h) \in \mathbb{R}^{8} \rbrace. $$

Note that the wedge product of two elements of \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{3})\) is non-commutative and non-associative.

Hodge Star

If you write the three-dimensional Hodge star operation in terms of the two-dimensional Hodge star operation and the two-dimensional conjugation operation, for

$$x = a + bW + cX + d (W \wedge X) + eY + f (W \wedge Y) + g (X \wedge Y) + h ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y);$$

you get

$${}* x = h + gW - fX - e (W \wedge X) + dY - c (W \wedge Y) + b (X \wedge Y) + a ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y).$$

However, this is not the Hodge star operation. Instead, it should be

$${}\star x = h + gW - fX + e (W \wedge X) + dY - c (W \wedge Y) + b (X \wedge Y) + a ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y).$$

That is:

$$ {}\star 1 = (W \wedge X) \wedge Y, \qquad {}\star ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) = 1; $$
$$ {}\star W = X \wedge Y, \qquad {}\star X = -W \wedge Y, \qquad {}\star Y = W \wedge X; $$
$$ {}\star (W \wedge X) = Y, \qquad {}\star (W \wedge Y) = -X, \qquad {}\star (X \wedge Y) = W. $$

Note that

$$ {}\star({}\star 1) = 1, \qquad {}\star ({}\star ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y)) = (W \wedge X) \wedge Y; $$
$$ {}\star({}\star W) = W, \qquad {}\star ({}\star X) = X, \qquad {}\star ({}\star Y) = Y; $$
$$ {}\star ({}\star (W \wedge X)) = W \wedge X, \qquad {}\star ({}\star (W \wedge Y)) = W \wedge Y, \qquad {}\star ({}\star (X \wedge Y)) = X \wedge Y. $$

That is, the three-dimensional Hodge star operation is an involution.

Four-Dimensional

In the four-dimensional case, the dimension of the exterior algebra is 16. This algebra can be obtained by performing a parabolic Cayley-Dickson construct with \(\Lambda(\mathbb{R}^{3})\). An element has the form

$$ x = x_{0} + x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + x_{4}; $$

where

$$ x_{0} = a_{0}; $$
$$ x_{1} = a_{W} W + a_{X} X + a_{Y} Y + a_{Z} Z; $$
$$ x_{2} = a_{WX} (W \wedge X) + a_{WY} (W \wedge Y) + a_{WZ} (W \wedge Z) + a_{XY} (X \wedge Y) + a_{XZ} (X \wedge Z) + a_{YZ} (Y \wedge Z); $$
$$ x_{3} = a_{WXY} ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) + a_{WXZ} ((W \wedge X) \wedge Z) + a_{WYZ} ((W \wedge Y) \wedge Z) + a_{XYZ} ((X \wedge Y) \wedge Z); $$
$$ x_{4} = a_{WXYZ} (((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) \wedge Z). $$

Hodge Star

In the four-dimensional case, the Hodge star operation provides a relation between 0-forms and 4-forms, a relation between 1-forms and 3-forms, and a relation between 2-forms. Explicitly, you have

$$ {}\star 1 = ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) \wedge Z; $$
$$ {}\star W = (X \wedge Y) \wedge Z, \qquad {}\star X = -(W \wedge Y) \wedge Z; $$
$$ {}\star Y = (W \wedge X) \wedge Z, \qquad {}\star Z = -(W \wedge X) \wedge Y; $$
$$ {}\star (W \wedge X) = Y \wedge Z, \qquad {}\star (W \wedge Y) = -X \wedge Z, \qquad {}\star (W \wedge Z) = X \wedge Y; $$
$$ {}\star (X \wedge Y) = W \wedge Z, \qquad {}\star (X \wedge Z) = -W \wedge Y, \qquad {}\star (Y \wedge Z) = W \wedge X; $$
$$ {}\star ((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) = Z, \qquad {}\star ((W \wedge X) \wedge Z) = -Y; $$
$$ {}\star ((W \wedge Y) \wedge Z) = X, \qquad {}\star ((X \wedge Y) \wedge Z) = -W; $$
$$ {}\star (((W \wedge X) \wedge Y) \wedge Z) = 1. $$

Again, the Hodge star operation is an involution.
